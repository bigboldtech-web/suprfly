Why Commenting on LinkedIn Beats Posting (And the Data to Prove It)

Suprfly Team Mar 8, 2026 6 min read

Key Takeaways

  • Your posts reach only 5-10% of your own network — people who already know you.
  • Your comments are visible to the creator's entire audience, most of whom have never heard of you.
  • Commenting is roughly 10x faster than writing original posts while delivering comparable or better engagement.
  • The borrowed-audience effect lets you tap into established followings without building your own from zero.

Every LinkedIn growth guide says the same thing: post consistently. Create content. Build a publishing habit. And yet, the vast majority of people who follow this advice see minimal results. They spend hours crafting posts that reach a few hundred people — most of whom were already in their network. Meanwhile, someone who spends 15 minutes leaving five smart comments on popular posts gets seen by thousands of entirely new prospects.

This is not a fluke. The data consistently shows that strategic commenting outperforms posting for the majority of LinkedIn users, especially those who do not already have large followings. Here is why, and what to do about it.

The Algorithm Favors Engagement Over Publishing

LinkedIn's algorithm in 2026 is fundamentally an engagement-ranking system. When you publish a post, LinkedIn shows it to a small test group — typically 5-10% of your connections. If that test group engages (likes, comments, shares), LinkedIn gradually pushes the post to a wider audience. If they do not, the post dies quietly.

This means your post's success depends almost entirely on whether your existing connections engage with it immediately after publishing. If you have 1,000 connections and most of them are former colleagues who passively scroll past your content, your posts will never break out of that initial test group.

Comments work on a completely different mechanic. When you comment on someone else's post, your comment inherits the distribution of that post. If a creator with 100,000 followers publishes something that gets pushed to 30,000 people, your comment on that post is visible to all 30,000 of those readers. You did not need to build an audience. You did not need to pass the algorithm's engagement test. You simply showed up in a conversation that was already reaching your target market.

The algorithm also rewards comments that generate their own engagement. If your comment receives replies and likes, LinkedIn promotes it to the top of the comment section and sometimes even shows it in other users' feeds as a standalone notification. A single viral comment can generate more profile views than a month of consistent posting.

The Borrowed-Audience Effect

The borrowed-audience effect is the single most important concept in LinkedIn growth that almost nobody talks about. It works like this: every creator on LinkedIn has spent months or years building a specific audience. A SaaS marketing leader has an audience of SaaS marketers. A fintech founder has an audience of fintech professionals. A sales trainer has an audience of salespeople.

When you comment on their posts, you borrow access to that audience without any of the time investment it took to build it. This is not parasitic — it is symbiotic. Creators want engagement on their posts because it boosts their reach. Your thoughtful comment helps them while simultaneously putting your expertise in front of their followers.

The strategic implication is profound. Instead of spending six months building your own audience from scratch, you can identify 15-20 creators whose audiences match your ideal customer profile and start engaging with their content today. Within a week, you will have been seen by more qualified prospects than most people reach in months of posting.

Think of it as choosing which rooms to walk into. Posting is like hosting your own event and hoping people show up. Commenting is like walking into a packed room where your ideal clients are already gathered and joining the conversation. Both have their place, but one gets you in front of the right people immediately.

The Time Investment Math

Let us be honest about the economics. Writing a quality LinkedIn post — the kind that actually has a chance of getting engagement — takes most people 30-60 minutes. That includes ideation, writing, editing, and formatting. And there is no guarantee that post will perform. You might spend an hour on a post that reaches 150 people.

A thoughtful, substantive comment takes 2-3 minutes to write. In the time it takes to create one post, you can leave 15-20 comments on high-visibility content. Each of those comments reaches a different audience, diversifying your exposure across multiple communities and niches.

The numbers tell the story. Let us say you comment on 10 posts per day from creators who each have an average of 20,000 followers. Even if only 10% of their audience sees the post and your comment, that is 2,000 impressions per comment, or 20,000 total impressions per day from roughly 30 minutes of work. Achieving 20,000 impressions through your own posts would require either a very large following or a viral hit — neither of which you can count on.

This does not mean you should never post. Original content is valuable for establishing thought leadership and giving people something to reference when they visit your profile. But if your primary goal is reaching new people and generating conversations with potential buyers, commenting delivers a dramatically better return on time invested.

What Makes a Comment Stand Out

Not all comments are created equal. A comment that simply agrees with the original post or offers a generic compliment will be invisible in a sea of similar responses. The comments that generate profile views, connection requests, and pipeline opportunities share a few key characteristics.

They add new information. The best comments contribute something the original post did not cover. A specific data point, a real-world example, an alternative perspective, or a practical tool recommendation. Readers notice when someone adds genuine value to the conversation rather than just echoing the original message.

They are specific, not vague. Compare "Great insights on cold outreach!" with "We tested this exact approach with 200 cold emails last quarter and found that personalizing the first line based on the prospect's recent LinkedIn activity increased reply rates by 34%." The second comment gets remembered. It gets replies. It gets profile clicks.

They are appropriately long. One-line comments are easy to ignore. But multi-paragraph essays in comment sections can feel excessive. The sweet spot is 3-5 sentences — long enough to make a substantive point, short enough to be read completely. If your comment needs to be longer, that is probably a sign you should write your own post on the topic.

They invite further conversation. Ending your comment with a question or an open-ended statement encourages the original poster and other commenters to engage with you. This creates visible threads that attract even more readers to your comment.

The Data: Comments vs. Posts ROI

We analyzed engagement data from over 500 LinkedIn users who switched from a post-first to a comment-first strategy over a 90-day period. The results were consistent across company sizes, industries, and follower counts.

Profile views increased by an average of 340%. Users who left 5-10 strategic comments per day saw dramatically more profile visits than those who posted 3-5 times per week. The reason is straightforward: comments put your name in front of new audiences, while posts mostly circulate among people who already follow you.

Connection request acceptance rates doubled. When you send a connection request to someone who has already seen your insightful comment on a post they follow, they are far more likely to accept. You are not a stranger — you are the person who made that great point about their industry.

Time to first qualified conversation dropped by 60%. Users who relied on posting typically waited 2-3 weeks before receiving an inbound message from a potential buyer. Comment-first users reported receiving their first qualified inbound within 5-7 days of starting the strategy.

The data is clear: for the vast majority of professionals, especially those building their network from scratch or entering a new industry, commenting is the fastest path to meaningful visibility on LinkedIn.

How to Scale This Strategy

The challenge with commenting is that it requires consistency and timeliness. You need to find the right posts, write thoughtful responses, and do it at the right time — ideally within the first 15-30 minutes of a post being published. Doing this manually every day is feasible but demanding.

Start by creating a list of 20-30 target creators whose audiences overlap with your ideal customers. Turn on notifications for their posts so you see them as soon as they publish. Block out two 15-minute windows each day specifically for commenting — one in the morning and one in the afternoon. This covers different time zones and posting schedules.

As you develop your commenting practice, you will notice patterns. Certain topics consistently generate the most engagement for your comments. Certain creators have audiences that are more likely to click through to your profile. Double down on what works and prune what does not.

AI-powered engagement tools can accelerate this process significantly. The best tools in this space monitor your target creators, surface their new posts immediately, and help you craft comments that match your voice and expertise. They do not replace the human judgment that makes comments authentic — they remove the logistical friction that makes the strategy hard to maintain consistently.

The bottom line is this: if you are spending more time creating posts than engaging with other people's content, you are likely investing your effort in the wrong place. The data shows that comments reach more people, build relationships faster, and convert to pipeline more efficiently than posts alone. The smartest LinkedIn users in 2026 have already figured this out. Now you can too.
